THAI TONE RULES

Before you start, you should already know some basics about the Thai alphabet. You should know all 44 consonants (as well as their pronunciation when they are at the end of a word) and all of the vowels (as well as their lengths). You should also know how to pronounce each of the 5 tones.

TONE MARKS

My Ayk These two tone marks are very common. It’s useful to know their names, so they are represented in the posters as a toe and an egg - two English words which sound similar to their names in Thai.
Mai Toh
(My toe)
Mai Dtree (always a high tone, so no symbol is required)
Mai Jud-ta-wah (always a rising tone, so no symbol is required)

CONSONANT CLASS

Diamond
(High Class)		 To make distinctions between high/mid/low class and high/mid/low tone, different objects represent the consonant classes. Here, high class is represented by an expensive object (a diamond), mid class by a mid-range object (a car) and low class by a cheap object (a hat). This way, you can still remember the technical terms, by relating them to the ‘value’ of the objects. (Now you can stop trying to memorize boring and confusing equations like “low class initial consonant + dead syllable + long vowel = falling tone” and learn them instead by using posters as visual reminders).

LIVE/DEAD SYLLABLE

Live syllable The smiley face represents a live syllable. The dead face represents a dead syllable.
Dead syllable
Live Syllables
Live syllables are ones which end in ‘sonorants’, also known as ‘singing sounds’ – sounds that you can drag out for a long time:

Consonants* (ng n m y w sounds)

ง น ม ย ว

OR a long vowel
Dead syllable
Dead syllables are ones which end in ‘stops’, sounds that you can’t drag out.

Consonants* (k p t sounds)

ก จ ด ต บ ป

OR a short vowel
*In Thai many consonants make a different depending on their position in a word. At the end of a word an ‘r’ becomes an ‘n’, a ‘j’ becomes a ‘t’ and so on.

VOWEL LENGTH

Short Vowel I have represented the short vowel as a short arrow, dead head and the long vowel as a long arrow, dead head. You will only need to know the vowel length for 1 out of the 15 tone rules, but it’s still worth remembering the vowel lengths. (Similar sounding words with different vowel lengths can have completely different meanings, regardless of tone).
